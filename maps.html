Processing Our Data

The first step in ArcGIS was to process our data to then be combined

Visualizations of our data sets are shown here. Each one was processed to have a field with a number between 0 and 1 where 0 is not a viable location for solar panels while 1 is the best

Energy Infrastructure Capability

The dark blue transparent areas represent areas close to major power lines. For the opaque areas, darker blue indicates a higher capability to transmit more power.

Alberta Elevation Aspect

The south facing slopes parts of Alberta have been identified as yellow, the flat parts as purple and the SW/SE parts as red, all others slopes are green.

Alberta Wildlife Risk

Areas that would have minimal risk to wildlife habitat are dark blue that is then on a scale to yellow that shows areas with critical to wildlife habitat or are unusable such as national parks, military bases are yellow.

Alberta Annular Solar Radiation

The average annual solar radiation is shown for Alberta where white indicates high levels of solar radiation while black indicates low levels.

Alberta Ecological Zones

Completely usable areas for solar panels are indicated by orange, partially usable areas are green and areas unable to be used such as forest or water is purple.

Combining Data


Using our raster values for each dataset they were multiplied together to find ideal locations to install a solar panel in Alberta and then processed to show the ideal data.

The maps are displayed on a spectrum where sliding from light yellow for poor spots to dark blue for ideal locations.

Identifying Key Areas

To finish our analysis we filtered out non ideal areas to create an actionable map of areas to identfy the best areas for potential installation of a solar panel farm.

Recording Our Steps

A key part in GIS projects is to ensure that the steps can be replicated. Some of the data we use is dynamic such as energy capability and annual solar radiation so this project needed to be designed to be rerun in the future.
